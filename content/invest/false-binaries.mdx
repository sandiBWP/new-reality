# False Binaries in Investing

The investment industry is organized around trade-offs. You can have returns *or* impact. You can go global *or* local. You can be diversified *or* concentrated. You can be an investor *or* a citizen.

What if many of these are not real trade-offs at all? What if they are failures of imagination — and missing mechanisms?

---

## Returns OR Impact

This is the most pervasive false binary in investing. The assumption is that competitive returns require disconnection from real-world outcomes — that caring about what your money does means accepting lower returns.

But this framing ignores a simple possibility: what if impact is not a concession but a diversification strategy? What if investments disconnected from regional economic reality carry risks that aren't being priced?

The question isn't returns *or* impact. It's: can we build structures that deliver both?

---

## Global OR Local

You don't have to choose. A portfolio can include both:

- Global exposure for growth, diversification, and liquidity
- Regional exposure for connection, resilience, and participation

The question isn't which one. It's: what's the right balance for your goals, your values, and the world you're living in?

---

## Growth OR Resilience

For decades, growth was the primary objective. Efficiency was the strategy. Scale was the reward.

But optimization for growth often removes the redundancy that creates resilience. A supply chain optimized for cost has no slack. A portfolio optimized for returns has no connection to place. A community optimized for efficiency has no buffer when conditions change.

The challenge isn't choosing between growth and resilience. It's designing systems that produce both.

---

## The Cost of False Choices

False binaries don't just limit thinking — they shape outcomes. When you accept that returns and impact are opposites, you never build products that deliver both. When you accept that global and local are mutually exclusive, you never create infrastructure that enables both.

The first step isn't picking a side. It's recognizing there was never supposed to be sides at all.
